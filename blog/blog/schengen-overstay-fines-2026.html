The Three Stages of an Overstay

When EES detects that you have overstayed your Schengen allowance, the consequences unfold in three stages. First, you are flagged in the database the moment your 90 days expire while you are still in Schengen. Second, when you attempt to exit or re-enter, border systems alert the officer immediately. Third, penalties are applied depending on the duration of your overstay and the country where you are caught.

Entry Bans: The Long-Term Damage

Financial penalties are painful but temporary. Entry bans are the real long-term consequence. An entry ban issued by any Schengen country applies automatically across all 29 member states simultaneously.

A ban of one to two years is typical for first-time overstays of fewer than 30 days. Overstays of 30 to 90 days typically result in three-year bans. Overstays exceeding 90 days or repeat violations can result in the maximum five-year ban.

During a ban, you cannot enter France for a weekend break, transit through Amsterdam's Schiphol Airport, or attend a business meeting in Berlin. The ban is total and immediate.

How to Avoid Overstaying

Track your days before every trip using the official rolling window calculation. Never estimate or use calendar months. Add both past trips and planned future trips to understand exactly where you stand.

If you realise you are approaching your limit while already in Schengen, leave before you reach 90 days. Voluntary departure before the limit is respected and carries no penalty. Waiting until you are caught carries significant consequences.

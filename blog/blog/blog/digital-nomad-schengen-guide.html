The 90/180 Problem for Digital Nomads

Most digital nomads discover the Schengen rule the hard way ‚Äî either when they are refused entry after a long trip, or when they realise they have been inadvertently working illegally. The rolling window calculation means that a six-month European summer can easily consume more than 90 days if you are not tracking carefully from the start.

The key insight most nomads miss is that the 90-day clock does not reset between countries. A month in Berlin followed by a month in Barcelona followed by a month in Lisbon is three months (roughly 90 days) total ‚Äî not three separate 30-day allowances.

The "90 In, 90 Out" Strategy

The most common strategy for digital nomads who want to spend significant time in Europe is to alternate 90-day periods inside and outside Schengen. Spend approximately 90 days in Schengen, then 90 days in non-Schengen countries before returning.

Popular non-Schengen destinations that work well for this strategy include the United Kingdom, Albania, Serbia, North Macedonia, Kosovo, Turkey, Georgia, Ukraine, and Morocco. Each of these countries offers its own visa-free or easy-access arrangements for most nationalities.

Digital Nomad Visas: The Legal Long-Stay Option

Several Schengen member states have introduced digital nomad visas that offer legal residency to remote workers, exempting them from the 90/180 rule entirely. If you earn income online and can demonstrate a minimum monthly income, these visas may be your best option.

Portugal D8 Digital Nomad Visa

Portugal offers one of the most accessible digital nomad visas in Europe. Requirements include proof of remote income of at least ‚Ç¨3,040 per month (four times the Portuguese minimum wage), health insurance, and a clean criminal record. The visa grants one year of residency renewable to five years, after which you can apply for permanent residency.

Spain Digital Nomad Visa

Spain launched its digital nomad visa in 2023 and has seen strong uptake. The minimum income requirement is approximately ‚Ç¨2,334 per month. The visa is initially valid for one year and can be renewed for up to five years. Spain's visa is particularly attractive due to its special tax regime for new residents.

Germany Freelancer Visa

Germany does not have a specific digital nomad visa but its freelancer visa (Freiberufler) works similarly for many professions including writers, designers, developers, and consultants. The application requires proof of clients or contracts and sufficient income to support yourself in Germany.

Tax Considerations for Nomads in Schengen

Spending significant time in any single Schengen country may trigger tax residency obligations in that country. Most countries consider you a tax resident if you spend more than 183 days there in a calendar year. Even without crossing this threshold, some countries have more aggressive tax residency rules based on where your economic interests are centred.

This is a complex area and the rules change frequently. Always consult a tax professional who specialises in international remote workers before spending extended periods in any Schengen country.
